Important Notice for EU Citizens

If you are a citizen of a country in the European Union and wish to remain in the United Kingdom after Brexit, you must apply for settled status; without this, your right to stay in the UK is not guaranteed.  The government had just launched a Settlement Scheme, offering EU citizens living here a legal route to remain.  To access information, go to www.gov.uk/settled-status-eu-citizens-families; information resources are available at www.gov.uk/government/publications/eu-settlement-scheme-community-leaders-toolkit.

The government announced last week that the fees which previously applied to the scheme have now been abolished, so that application will be free.

The scheme does not apply to Irish citizens.

If you know of anyone who is an EU citizen and may not have access to this information, please make them aware.

We must serve God both by external acts and by internal acts.  We are possessed of a double nature, we are intellectual beings and sentient beings also.  We should therefore offer to God a double adoration – a spiritual adoration, consisting in the interior devotion of the mind, and a bodily adoration made up of the external humiliation of the body.  And since in all acts done in acknowledgement that God is God the external acts depend on the internal – for the internal act is the more important – so the external acts of adoration are done for the sake of the internal adoration.  That is to say, by our gestures of humility that we are moved to subject ourselves to God in our inclinations and our will.  This is due to our nature being what it is, for it is natural to man to proceed to things that can only be known through the intelligence from the starting point of things seen, felt, hear and known by the senses.

So, just as prayer has its origin in something in the mind, and is only in the second place expressed in words, adoration also consists, primarily and in its origin, in an internal reverence of God and only secondarily in certain bodily signs that we are humbling ourselves: such bodily signs, for example, mas genuflections to show our weakness by comparison with God, or prostrations to show that we are nothing of ourselves.

 

St Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274)
